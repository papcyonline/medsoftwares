## Understanding Compounding Pharmacy Regulations 2026

### USP Standards Overview

**USP &lt;795&gt;: Pharmaceutical Compounding—Nonsterile Preparations**
- Standards for compounding nonsterile medications
- Beyond-use dating (BUD) requirements
- Quality control and testing
- Facility and equipment standards
- Personnel training and competency
- **Status:** Current version enforceable

**USP &lt;797&gt;: Pharmaceutical Compounding—Sterile Preparations**
- Standards for sterile compounding (IV admixtures, injections, ophthalmics)
- Cleanroom classification requirements (ISO Class 5, 7, 8)
- Environmental monitoring and testing
- Personnel gowning and aseptic technique
- Media fill testing and competency assessment
- **Status:** Revised version effective, with continued updates

**USP &lt;800&gt;: Hazardous Drugs—Handling in Healthcare Settings**
- Standards for handling antineoplastics and other hazardous drugs
- Receipt, storage, compounding, dispensing, administration, disposal
- Containment primary and secondary engineering controls
- Personal protective equipment (PPE) requirements
- Environmental monitoring and wipe sampling
- **Status:** Enforceable by FDA as of November 1, 2023; state boards enforcing

### FDA Oversight and Enforcement

**Current Regulatory Framework:**
- **503A Pharmacies:** Traditional compounding under state pharmacy board oversight
- **503B Outsourcing Facilities:** Register with FDA, subject to cGMP requirements
- **FDA Inspections:** Increasing frequency for both 503A and 503B
- **Adverse Event Reporting:** Mandatory for serious complications
- **Product Recall Authority:** FDA can mandate recalls for non-compliant products

### State Board Requirements

**Licensure and Inspections:**
- Specialized compounding pharmacy licenses (many states)
- Annual or biennial inspections
- USP 795, 797, 800 compliance verification
- Personnel competency documentation
- Facility design and environmental control validation

## USP 800 Hazardous Drug Compliance

### Defining Hazardous Drugs

**NIOSH List Drugs:**
- Group 1: Antineoplastic drugs
- Group 2: Non-antineoplastic hazardous drugs (hormones, antivirals, immunosuppressants)
- Group 3: Drugs with reproductive risk

**Assessment Tools:**
- NIOSH hazardous drug list integration into software
- Facility-specific assessment for additional drugs
- Automatic flagging of hazardous drugs in orders

### Containment Requirements

**Primary Engineering Controls (PECs):**
- Biological safety cabinets (BSCs)
- Compounding aseptic containment isolators (CACIs)
- Software tracking which PEC used for each compound

**Secondary Engineering Controls (SECs):**
- Buffer rooms (ISO Class 7 or 8 depending on garb)
- Negative pressure relative to surrounding areas
- Dedicated hazardous drug compounding areas

**Software Tracking:**
- Assignment of compounds to specific PECs/SECs
- Equipment certification expiration alerts
- Maintenance scheduling
- Cleaning and decontamination documentation

### Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

**Required PPE Documentation:**
- Chemotherapy gloves (double gloving for high-risk)
- Gowns (disposable, impervious)
- Shoe covers
- Hair covers
- Eye/face protection
- Respiratory protection for certain scenarios

**Software Features:**
- PPE compliance checklists
- Training documentation for proper donning/doffing
- Inventory management for PPE supplies

### Surface Wipe Sampling

**USP 800 Requirement:**
- Routine sampling of surfaces in hazardous drug compounding areas
- At least twice per year (monthly for new compounders or after facility changes)
- Lab analysis for drug residue
- Action levels established

**Software Management:**
- Sampling location maps
- Scheduled sampling reminders
- Lab order and result tracking
- Trending and analytics
- Action level exceedance alerts
- Corrective action planning and documentation

## Implementing Compounding Pharmacy Software

### Phase 1: Needs Assessment (Month 1)

**Evaluate:**
- Current compounding volume (prescriptions/day, batches/week)
- Types of compounding (sterile vs. non-sterile, hazardous vs. non-hazardous)
- Regulatory requirements (503A vs. 503B, state-specific)
- Existing technology (pharmacy management system, automated compounding devices)
- Pain points (compliance burden, documentation time, errors, efficiency)

**Define Requirements:**
- Must-have features vs. nice-to-have
- Integration needs with existing systems
- User count (compounders, pharmacists, technicians)
- Budget constraints
- Implementation timeline

### Phase 2: Vendor Selection (Months 1-2)

**Evaluate Platforms:**
- Request demos from Simplifi+, PK Software, LogiCoy, and others
- Assess ease of use and learning curve
- Verify USP 795/797/800 compliance capabilities
- Check integration with your pharmacy system
- Review customer references from similar pharmacies
- Compare pricing (subscription vs. perpetual, per-user fees)

**Selection Criteria:**
- Feature completeness
- User experience
- Vendor support and training quality
- Track record and customer satisfaction
- Total cost of ownership

### Phase 3: Implementation and Configuration (Months 2-4)

**Technical Setup:**
- Install software (cloud vs. on-premise)
- Configure system settings and user accounts
- Integrate with pharmacy management system
- Connect barcode scanners, label printers, scales

**Data Migration:**
- Import master formula library
- Transfer ingredient inventory
- Load historical batch records (if converting from another system)
- Upload SOPs and documentation templates

**Customization:**
- Configure formulas specific to your pharmacy
- Set up beyond-use dating rules
- Customize labels and batch records
- Define quality control workflows
- Establish user roles and permissions

### Phase 4: Training (Months 3-4)

**Staff Training:**
- Compounders: Formula selection, batch record completion, ingredient verification
- Pharmacists: Formula approval, final verification, quality assurance
- Technicians: Inventory management, label printing, order entry
- Quality assurance: Environmental monitoring, competency tracking, reporting

**Training Methods:**
- Vendor-provided training sessions (onsite or virtual)
- Hands-on practice with test compounds
- Competency assessment before go-live
- Quick reference guides and job aids
- Ongoing training for new features

### Phase 5: Go-Live and Optimization (Months 4-6)

**Soft Launch:**
- Start with non-sterile compounding or low-risk sterile products
- Parallel run with manual processes initially (if feasible)
- Monitor closely for issues
- Gather user feedback daily

**Full Launch:**
- Expand to all compounding operations
- Transition fully to electronic batch records
- Discontinue paper-based processes
- Establish regular quality audits of software data

**Continuous Improvement:**
- Regular review of formulas and updates
- Software version upgrades
