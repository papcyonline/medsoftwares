## What is an EMR?

### Definition

An **Electronic Medical Record (EMR)** is a digital version of the paper chart in a single healthcare facility. It contains medical and treatment history from one practice.

### What EMRs Include

- Patient demographics
- Medical history (at that facility)
- Diagnoses and treatment plans
- Medications prescribed
- Lab results
- Clinical notes
- Billing information

### EMR Characteristics

**Internal Focus:**
- Data stays within the facility
- Optimized for internal workflow
- May not easily share data externally

**Provider-Centric:**
- Designed around clinician needs
- Supports clinical decision-making
- Tracks patient progress over time

**Limitations:**
- Records can't easily follow patients
- Information silos between providers
- Manual effort to share records

### Who Uses EMRs

- Single-physician practices
- Small clinics
- Specialty practices
- Facilities not needing external data exchange

## What is an EHR?

### Definition

An **Electronic Health Record (EHR)** is a comprehensive digital record designed to be shared across multiple healthcare settings. It's a broader view of a patient's overall health.

### What EHRs Include

Everything in an EMR, plus:
- Records from multiple providers
- Patient-contributed data
- Health information exchanges (HIE)
- Care coordination data
- Population health information

### EHR Characteristics

**Interoperability:**
- Designed for data sharing
- Connects multiple providers
- Follows patients across care settings

**Patient-Centric:**
- Complete health picture
- Supports coordinated care
- Enables patient access (portals)

**Advanced Features:**
- Decision support across contexts
- Population health analytics
- Quality measure reporting

### Who Uses EHRs

- Health systems
- Hospital networks
- Accountable Care Organizations
- Facilities requiring care coordination
- Government-mandated settings

## Key Differences Explained

### 1. Data Scope

| EMR | EHR |
|-----|-----|
| Your facility's data only | Data from many facilities |
| What happened HERE | What happened EVERYWHERE |
| Practice-level view | Patient-level view |

**Example:**
- EMR shows: "Patient visited your clinic on Jan 15"
- EHR shows: "Patient visited your clinic Jan 15, ER on Jan 12, specialist on Jan 8"

### 2. Interoperability

| EMR | EHR |
|-----|-----|
| May not share data | Built for sharing |
| Proprietary formats | Standard formats (HL7, FHIR) |
| Manual data exchange | Automated exchange |

**Why It Matters:**
Without interoperability, you're treating patients with incomplete information. EHRs aim to solve this.

### 3. Patient Access

| EMR | EHR |
|-----|-----|
| Limited patient access | Patient portals standard |
| Provider-controlled | Patient participates |
| One-way information | Two-way communication |

### 4. Care Coordination

| EMR | EHR |
|-----|-----|
| Supports internal care | Supports cross-provider care |
| Referrals are manual | Referrals with data |
| Care gaps possible | Comprehensive view |

## Which Do You Need?

### Choose EMR-Focused If:

- You're a single-location practice
- You don't participate in health information exchanges
- Your patients don't need records shared elsewhere
- Budget is the primary concern
- Simple clinical documentation is sufficient

**Recommended:** Small independent practices, specialty clinics

### Choose EHR-Focused If:

- You're part of a health system
- You need to share data with other providers
- You participate in quality reporting programs
- Care coordination is important
- Patients see multiple providers

**Recommended:** Hospitals, health networks, ACOs

## The Evolution: From EMR to EHR

### Phase 1: Paper to Digital (EMR)

**Timeline:** 1990s-2000s

Focus was on:
- Digitizing paper charts
- Improving legibility
- Internal efficiency
- Basic clinical documentation

### Phase 2: Connectivity (EHR)

**Timeline:** 2010s

Focus shifted to:
- Meaningful Use requirements
- Health information exchanges
- Care coordination
- Patient engagement

### Phase 3: Integrated Care (Modern)

**Timeline:** 2020s-Present

Current state:
- Interoperability mandates
- Patient data access rights
- API-based connectivity

## Common Misconceptions

### "EMR and EHR are the same thing"

**Reality:** They serve different purposes. An EMR is a record; an EHR is a system for sharing records.

### "You must have an EHR to be compliant"

**Reality:** Requirements vary by region and facility type. Many small practices operate fine with EMR-only solutions.

### "EHRs are always more expensive"

**Reality:** Cost depends on implementation, not necessarily EMR vs EHR distinction. Solutions like HospitalOS offer both capabilities affordably.

### "Interoperability is automatic with any EHR"

**Reality:** Interoperability requires standards compliance, active connections, and often additional setup.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can you have an EHR without an EMR?

Not really. An EHR is built on EMR functionality. You need the medical record before you can share it.

### Is one better than the other?

Neither is "better"—they serve different purposes. The best solution depends on your specific needs.

### Are there legal differences?

Both must comply with healthcare data regulations (HIPAA, etc.). EHRs have additional interoperability requirements in some jurisdictions.

### Can I upgrade from EMR to EHR?

Many systems grow with you. HospitalOS includes both capabilities from the start.

### Do patients care about EMR vs EHR?

Patients care about access to their records and coordinated care—both more likely with EHR capabilities.
